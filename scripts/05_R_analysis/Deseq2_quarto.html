Comparative RNA-seq Analysis of SMARCE1 and NF2 Loss in Meningioma

Author

Glady Hazitha Samuel

Introduction

Background on SMARCE1

Clear cell meningiomas are rare and aggressive brain tumors that are characterized by loss of function mutations in SMARCE1 (St. Pierre et al. 2022; Smith et al. 2013). SMARCE1 is a key component of the BAF complex, a chromosome remodelling complex (Kadoch and Crabtree 2015). During transcription, the BAF complex regulates gene expression by altering chromosome structure to allow or restrict access to transcription factors, enhancers and other molecules involved in transcription (Kadoch and Crabtree 2015). Mutations in components of the BAF complex are often associated with cancers (Shain and Pollack 2013). Although many of the components of the BAF complex have been well studied, the specific role of SMARCE1 in transcriptional regulation and gene expression is unclear.

In this Nature Genetics publication, the authors study the role of SMARCE1 in context of the BAF complex (St. Pierre et al. 2022). Here the authors use various analysis including RNA-seq, ATAC-seq and ChIP-seq to understand this pathway and possible pharmaceutical interventions.

Data and Study Design

This study uses a combination of RNA-seq, ATAC-seq and ChIP-seq data to study the mechanism by which loss of SMARCE1 results in tumorogenesis. The authors performed comprehensive transcriptomic analysis of SMARCE Null cells compared to WT controls in AC7 arachnoid cells which are used as a meningioma model. They also looked at NF2 deficient AC7 cells which are involved in many fibrous meningiomas.In this project, I specifically focus on the RNASeq analysis and study the key biological pathways involved with loss of SMARCE1 and NF2 to look at pathways that are shared and distinct between the two conditions.The study includes libraries prepared using Illumina Tru-Seq Stranded mRNA Library Prep Kit. Using a AC7 meningioma cell model, clones were established for WT(3 clones-2 replicates each – 6 biological replicates) and SMARCE1 Null(2 Clones- 2 replicates each = 4 biological replicates.). The reads are single end reads of 75bp length sequenced on Illumina NextSeq 500.

RNA-seq Workflow

The authors of this paper used STAR and the hg19 human gene annotation file for alignments. The output gene count tables which were then loaded into EdgeR (v.3.12.1) to evaluate differential gene expression. Downstream, the EdgeR log2Fold changes were used for GSEA analysis using the GSEAPreranked Tool (GSEA63). For enrichment of select genes, Metscape or Hypergeomatric tests were used to see which pathways were enriched amongst the select subset of genes. Heatmaps were made based on RPKM values which were convered to z-scores. PCA was performed to show sample level differences using functions from the corpcor package in R.

Results

The PCA analysis of the samples show a strong separation between the NF2 null and the SMARCE1 null gene expression. In comparison to the WT, the authors found 3456 differentially expressed genes in SMARCE1 Null cells. Of these, 1684 were significantly downregulated and 1772 were upregulated. The paper does not list the numbers for differentially expressed genes in the NF2 null cells. However, in a combined heatmap, the figure shows that 4226 genes were significantly differentially expressed in either SMARCE1 null of NF2 null compared to WT. The full list of differentially expressed genes is not available however, figures show representative genes and categories associated with each condition. These genes are differentially expressed in both Smarce Null and NF2 null compared to WT: FLNA, SOD2, CREB1, SKP2 and STXBP1.

RNA-seq Analysis

QC and Alignment

For the intital QC analysis, Fastqc and Multiqc analysis were performed the for single read RNA-seq libraries from the publication. The total number of sequences per sample ranged form 14 million to 60 millions reads. The mean quality phred scores were above 30 for all samples. 4 samples failed the per base sequence content and 1 sample failed the per base GC content. The duplication levels were also high and 36/46 samples failed according to the multiqc. However on further research in the literature, this seems normal for RNAseq data. Overall, the adapter content was low for all samples and all samples passed QC. However, since a few samples showed slight increase in adapters in the 3’ end, I performed adapter trimming. After trimming, the QC report showed the expected improvements. Adapter content was mostly eliminated across all samples, and the per-base quality profile became much more uniform along the read length. Overrepresented sequences also decreased substantially after trimming, as well as the GC content. The main metric that remained unchanged was the duplication levels. Overall, the trimming step effectively addressed the issues I saw in the raw data. This publication did not go into details regarding the initial QC and trimming steps etc. This could be because it included a variety of omics analysis including RNAseq, CHIPseq ,ATACseq and scRNAseq which could be why they did not emphasize the analysis details/methods for RNAseq.

Import Count Data

The gene count data from HTSeq were imported and organized into a DESeqDataSet with the experimental design ~ condition to enable differential expression testing between SMARCE1-null, NF2-null, and wild-type samples.

Quality Control and Filtering

Wild-type was set as the reference condition for all comparisons. Genes with < than 50 total counts across all samples were filtered out to reduce noise and improve statistical power. The histogram shows the distribution of the log transformed counts.

DESEQ dds

Differential expression testing was performed using DESeq2, which models count data with a negative binomial distribution to account for biological and technical variability. The pipeline estimates normalization factors, dispersion parameters, and performs statistical testing to identify genes with significant expression changes between conditions

Differentially Expressed Genes

The Differential expression analysis identified 8,037 genes significantly changed in SMARCE1-null cells and 4,067 genes in NF2-null cells (adjusted p-value < 0.1) as seen in the table below. SMARCE1 loss affected nearly twice as many genes as NF2 loss, suggesting a broader transcriptional impact. In the SMARCE1-responsive genes, 3,985 were upregulated and 4,052 were downregulated,showing a balanced transcriptional response with slightly more gene repression. For NF2, 2,103 genes were upregulated and 1,964 downregulated, showing a similar balance between activation and repression. The comparable ratios up and downregulated genes in both conditions suggest that loss of either SMARCE1 or NF2 triggers both activating and repressive transcriptional programs, rather than a unidirectional effect.

DESEQ2 LFC shrinkage

In order to get more accurate effect size estimates for visualizations, a log2 fold change shrinkage method called ashr was used. This improves individual gene-level estimates by reducing noise for downstream visualizations and analysis such as GSEA.

MA PLOTS

MA plots visualize the relationship between mean normalized count and log2 fold change. Significantly differentially expressed genes (padj < 0.1) are highlighted in blue. Based on the plots below we can see that the shrinkage is working correctly.

Ensembl gene IDs were annotated with human gene symbols using the org.Hs.eg.db database to facilitate biological interpretation of results.

Volcano Plot

Volcano plots visualize the relationship between log2 fold change and -log10 adjusted p-value for all genes. Genes are color-coded by significance where red indicates both statistically significant (padj < 0.1) and biologically meaningful (|log2FC| > 1) changes, while gray represents non-significant genes. The top 15 most significant genes are labeled with gene symbols. SMARCE1 loss affected 8,037 genes while NF2 loss affected 4,067 genes, with both conditions showing similar amounts of up and downregulated genes.

Principal Component Analysis

PCA is an important QC figure and it shows the overall relationships of the samples based on gene expression clustering. In our analysis, the first two principal componenets captured about 69% of the variability in gene expression. PC1 separates the WT from the SMARCE1 null while the NF2 null samples are positioned between the WT and SMARCE1 Null, suggesting a gene expression signature that is intermediate between WT and SMARCE1.

Heatmap Visualization

To visualize expression patterns of the most differentially expressed genes, heatmaps were generated of the top 30 genes ranked by absolute log2 fold change. Gene expression was displayed using regularized log-transformed (rlog) counts, which stabilize variance across different expression levels.

Three versions of the heatmap were created: 1. Raw rlog counts : Shows absolute expression levels across all samples 2. baseMean-scaled : Normalizes by average expression to emphasize relative changes 3. WT-scaled : Centers expression relative to wild-type samples to highlight deviations from control

From the plots below, we see that the SMARCE1 clustering is distinct from the WT , while NF2 shows an intermediate clustering which falls between WT and SMARCE1 for the top regulated genes. The plots also shows that the genes are both up and downregulated in the knockdown, suggesting there is an overall transcriptional change which is not directional.

Heatmaps were also generated for the NF2 null vs WT comparison to visualize the top 20 genes in each condition.

GO Analysis

Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis identified the biological processes significantly overrepresented among differentially expressed genes in each knockout condition.

According to the plots below, SMARCE1 loss primarily affects developmental and structural processes wuch as skeletal development, tissue morphogenesis, and extracellular matrix metabolism. The enrichment of negative regulation of cell proliferation suggests disruption of growth control mechanisms whic is consistent with tumor development. These pathways fit with SMARCE1’s role in chromatin remodeling during development.

NF2 loss activates immune and inflammatory signaling pathways—defense responses, cytokine production, and cellular signaling through protein phosphorylation. This is very different from SMARCE1, suggesting that NF2 mutations trigger an immune response and not developmental dysregulation.

These completely different GO profiles suggest that these two genes drive tumorgenesis through different mechanisms. This also fits with the fact that they are associated with different subtypes of meningiomas.

Discussion

In this section we will talk about the key findings of the RNA-seq analysis and compare it with the published data.

Key Findings 1. Smarce1 Null cells hows a transcriptional signature that is distinct from WT cells. 2. Loss of Smarce1 results in about half the genes upregulated and half downregulated, suggesting an overall transcriptional activation and repression that is not directional. 3. NF2 null cells also show a similar pattern, however only 3456 genes were differentially expressed compared to Smarce Null cells which had 8037 genes differentially expressed. 4. The PCA plot shows strong and distinct clustering of the Smarce Null and nF2 null suggesting they are transcriptionally different although both are involved in tumorogenesis.

Our analysis identified a larger number of differentially expressed genes (8,037 vs 3,456 for SMARCE1) compared to the original publication where 1,684 genes were significantly downregulated1,772 genes were significantly upregulated. This is likely due to differences in statistical thresholds (padj < 0.05 compared to stricter cut offs), the software versions (DESeq2 vs edgeR v3.12.1) and filtering approaches. However, the ratio of upregulated to downregulated genes remained consistent with the published results, confirming the balanced transcriptional response to SMARCE1 loss.
